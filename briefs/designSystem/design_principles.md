# Andrew Whited — Design Principles

## Purpose
This site should present Andrew Whited’s studio, objects, art, image work, and store within one coherent system. It should feel authored, editorial, and culturally literate while remaining clear, usable, and commercially functional where needed.

The site should not feel like a template portfolio, a generic e-commerce storefront, or a startup-branded personal site. It should feel deliberate, spatial, typographic, and image-led.

---

## Overall Feeling
The site should feel:

- editorial, not corporate
- refined, not cliché-luxury
- minimal, but not empty
- rational, but not sterile
- image-forward
- calm, spacious, and paced
- thoughtful and authored
- culturally literate
- commercially capable without becoming storefront-first

Warmth should come from the work itself: images, materials, subjects, references, and writing. It should not come from decorative branding gestures or trend-driven softness.

---

## Core Principles

### 1. Editorial over corporate
The site should feel closer to an editorial or cultural presentation than a business website. It should frame the work with clarity and intent rather than sounding promotional or over-explained.

### 2. Restraint over decoration
The visual system should rely on spacing, typography, image placement, and proportion rather than ornament. Design should emerge from order, rhythm, and hierarchy.

### 3. Rational structure with human warmth
The system should be highly ordered and grid-based, but not cold. Rational structure should create calm and clarity, while images and content provide warmth, texture, and life.

### 4. Image-led experience
Images should carry much of the site’s atmosphere and pacing. Photography should do more emotional work than UI styling.

### 5. Typography as architecture
Type should do a significant amount of the design work. Hierarchy, rhythm, pacing, and tone should come primarily from typography and spacing rather than graphic embellishment.

### 6. Distinct modes within one system
Studio, Objects, Art, Image, and Store should feel like parts of the same site, but each should have a distinct role and behavior. The design system should unify them without flattening them into sameness.

### 7. Commerce without dilution
The site should support selling work, but commerce should not dominate the entire experience. Product pages can become more utilitarian, but the overall site should remain editorial and authored.

### 8. Systems over novelty
Reusable patterns, consistent logic, and content structure should drive the design. The site should feel intentional and coherent rather than full of one-off gestures.

---

## Structural Constraints

- The top navigation is always present and floats over hero imagery when present.
- Navigation should feel integrated into the design, not like a separate utility bar.
- Home is reached by clicking the logo — it does not appear as a labeled nav item.
- Navigation order is fixed: Studio · Objects · Art · Image · Store.
- The site must be fully responsive. Layout, navigation, typography, image handling, and spacing must hold their logic across screen sizes.

---

## Anti-Principles
The site should avoid feeling like:

- Squarespace template minimalism
- a startup portfolio
- a generic creative studio site
- a DTC furniture brand with soft lifestyle branding
- a crowded marketplace
- faux-premium luxury minimalism
- a gallery site that sacrifices usability for mood
- an over-animated “creative developer” project
- a try-hard brutalist experiment
- a cold formal exercise with no life in it

References and influences include:

- Donald Judd

- Dora Lazarevic
- Wim Crouwel

- Minimalism

These references should inform the site’s structure, discipline, typographic clarity, and sense of order. They should not be copied literally or turned into a pastiche.

---

## Layout and Grid Principles

- The layout should be strongly grid-based.
- Baseline-grid thinking should inform rhythm and vertical spacing.
- Asymmetry is welcome, but it should be disciplined and intentional.
- Negative space should be active and meaningful, not just sparse.
- Proportion and scale should feel harmonious.
- The site should feel structured enough to create calm.
- Layout decisions should come from a system, not visual improvisation.

When generating layouts or components, prioritize:

1. typography system
2. spacing system
3. grid structure

Avoid arbitrary values that do not follow these systems.

The site should feel designed through alignment, proportion, spacing, and interval.

---

## Typography Principles

- Typography should be sophisticated, disciplined, and rational.
- Type hierarchy should be clear and deliberate.
- Scales and ratios should matter.
- The typography should feel confident, not ornamental.
- The site should privilege clarity, spacing, and rhythm over decorative type treatments.
- Type should help establish tone and distinction across sections.
- Text should be easy to scan but still feel elegant and considered.

Typography should feel like structure, not styling.

---

## Image Principles

- Images should be given room.
- Image placement should help determine pace and emphasis.
- Photography should provide warmth, atmosphere, and material specificity.
- Images should not feel like generic filler inside a template layout.
- Cropping, sequencing, and spacing should feel intentional.
- The site should support visual immersion without becoming chaotic.

For Image, the experience can feel lighter and more gallery-like.  
For Objects and Art, imagery should support both meaning and material detail.

---

## Interaction Principles

- Interaction should be quiet and precise.
- Motion should be subtle and restrained.
- Hover states, transitions, and reveals should support hierarchy, not distract from it.
- The site should feel responsive and polished, but not gimmicky.
- Navigation should feel simple and confident.
- Users should always understand where they are and how to move through the site.

Interaction should reinforce calm and clarity.

---

## Commerce Principles

- Commerce should feel integrated into the site rather than bolted on.
- The store should align visually with the rest of the site, but it may become slightly more utilitarian at the product-page level.
- Product pages should hold the deep detail needed for evaluation and purchase.
- The store should never overpower the editorial and cultural framing of the site.

### Objects and Store
Objects is the editorial discovery layer.  
Store is the canonical detail and transaction layer for purchasable objects.

This means:
- Objects pages frame collections, context, references, and discovery.
- Store product pages hold detailed photos, dimensions, attributes, stock state, and purchase actions.

### Art
Art is the canonical presentation layer for artworks.

This means:
- One-of-one works should live fully under Art.
- Art work pages can include price, status, and inquiry.
- Original artworks do not need duplicate store listings unless they also exist as editions, prints, publications, or other products.

---

## Section-Specific Tone

### Studio
Studio should feel interpretive, contextual, and personal. It introduces the practice, the workshop, services, and references.

### Objects
Objects should feel editorial and guidebook-like. Collections should provide context, references, places, and framing for the work. Core and Commissions can be more direct.

### Art
Art should feel more singular and focused. Each work should have enough space to feel like an artwork, not a SKU.

### Image
Image should feel visual-first, fast, and immersive. The grid and lightbox behavior should support browsing without adding unnecessary conceptual weight.

### Store
Store should feel clear, functional, and coherent with the rest of the site. It can become more practical, but should not feel like a generic e-commerce template.

---

## Practical Takeaway for Implementation
When making design decisions, prefer:

- order over flourish
- typography over decoration
- spacing over embellishment
- systems over one-off moments
- image quality and pacing over interface noise
- editorial clarity over promotional language
- visual continuity over disconnected page types

The final site should feel like one authored system with different modes, not a portfolio, blog, gallery, and store awkwardly stitched together.
